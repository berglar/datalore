Rewriting the life code

Hacking The Genome

Can we rewrite the code of life? Scientists have discovered a breakthrough tool called CRISPR that will allow us to edit our DNA.

We’re here to recruit the best and the brightest for the inaugural genomeHack, which will bring together self-hackers, geneticists, Big Data researchers and data visualization experts to learn to rewrite our own genomes. We want you, some of the world’s most innovative thinkers, to give this new technology a test drive. If you like it, we’re confident you’ll spread the word to others.

Millions of us track our heart rates, exercise, diet, and gut microbes. We share and “like” our genetic readouts on Genomebook and DNAMatch.com. We no longer think twice about those ubiquitous DNA security scanners in the lobbies of government buildings. Reading our genes is as common as testing cholesterol was in the 20th century -- and as cheap as recharging our car twice a week. This has led to drugs precisely targeted to the specific mutations in our DNA that cause diseases. Thanks to the transformative new technology CRISPR-- we canedit the genome to replace disease-causing mutations, making the current generation the first that can expect to live to 100 -- or longer. Timeline

For most of the 20th century, starting from the discovery of DNA’s double-helix structure in 1953, genome science focused on reading DNA sequences. It took more than a decade, and billions of dollars, for the Human Genome Project to decipher the sequence of a single person.

But the technology got rapidly better. Machines can now sequence the full human genome in minutes, for well under $1,000. The arrival of CRISPR in 2013 as an editing tool for the human genome transformed genomics. It made it possible to rapidly and easily write genetic code, opening the possibility of fixing mutations that cause or raise the risk of disease.

Hacking Potentials

CRISPR technology was hacked from bacteria. It serves as an immune system for bacteria to defend itself against harmful viruses and plasmids by recognizing and cutting these extraneous pieces of DNA. In 2013, scientists demonstrated that this tool could be hijacked to precisely edit the genomes of other organisms, including humans. Researchers can now customize the CRISPR machinery to search for hand-picked short sequences of DNA throughout the genome, delete unwanted segments that cause or increase the risk of disease, and even insert new DNA sequences that could cure diseases. For example, using this approach, knocking out the CCR5 in blood cells can prevent infection by the HIV virus, conferring immunity. The technology is not yet perfect. It occasionally makes cuts in the wrong place. But it’s good enough, and safe enough, for you to begin rewriting your own genome.
