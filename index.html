Rewriting the life code

Hacking The Genome

Millions of us track our heart rates, exercise, diet, and gut microbes. We share and “like” our genetic readouts on Genomebook and DNAMatch.com. We no longer think twice about those ubiquitous DNA security scanners in the lobbies of government buildings. Reading our genes is as common as testing cholesterol was in the 20th century -- and as cheap as recharging our car twice a week. This has led to drugs precisely targeted to the specific mutations in our DNA that cause diseases. Thanks to the transformative new technology CRISPR-- we can edit the genome to replace disease-causing mutations, making the current generation the first that can expect to live to 100 -- or longer. Join us at the first genomeHack to learn how to rewrite your own DNA code.

Hacking Potentials

CRISPR technology was hacked from bacteria. It serves as an immune system for bacteria to defend itself against harmful viruses and plasmids by recognizing and cutting these extraneous pieces of DNA. In 2013, scientists demonstrated that this tool could be hijacked to precisely edit the genomes of other organisms, including humans. Researchers can now customize the CRISPR machinery to search for hand-picked short sequences of DNA throughout the genome, delete unwanted segments that cause or increase the risk of disease, and even insert new DNA sequences that could cure diseases. For example, using this approach, knocking out the CCR5 in blood cells can prevent infection by the HIV virus, conferring immunity. The technology is not yet perfect. It occasionally makes cuts in the wrong place. But it’s good enough, and safe enough, for you to begin rewriting your own genome.
